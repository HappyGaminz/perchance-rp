The Yarlung Tsangpo Grand Canyon in Tibet is recognized as the deepest canyon on land in the world.

Reaching a maximum depth of approximately 6,009 meters, the Tsangpo Canyon is over three times deeper than many other famous gorges.

The Yarlung Tsangpo stretches for about 504 kilometers, making it one of the longest river-cut canyons on the planet.

This Grand Canyon of the East was formed by the Yarlung Tsangpo River as it cuts through the Himalayan mountain range.

The Tsangpo Gorge creates a dramatic "U-turn" known as the Great Bend around the 7,782-meter Namcha Barwa peak.

Mount Namcha Barwa and Gyala Peri stand on either side of the Yarlung Tsangpo Grand Canyon, creating its immense vertical walls.

Due to its extreme whitewater and dangerous terrain, the Tsangpo River section within the gorge is nicknamed the "Everest of Rivers."

The Yarlung Tsangpo ecosystem ranges from subtropical jungles at the bottom to arctic conditions at the surrounding peaks.

Rare species like the takin and the Bengal tiger have been documented within the remote reaches of the Tsangpo Canyon.

In 2023, scientists discovered Asia’s tallest tree, a 102-meter cypress, living inside the Yarlung Tsangpo Grand Canyon.

The Tsangpo contains several massive waterfalls, including the 30-meter "Hidden Falls" which were only confirmed by outsiders in the late 1990s.

Tibetan Buddhists consider the Yarlung Tsangpo Grand Canyon a "beyul," or a sacred hidden land of spiritual significance.

The Yarlung Tsangpo river eventually flows into India, where it becomes the Brahmaputra River.

Explorers in the 19th and early 20th centuries struggled to map the Tsangpo Gorge because the terrain was so impenetrable.

The Yarlung Tsangpo Grand Canyon experiences a massive drop in elevation, falling thousands of meters over a relatively short distance.

Many parts of the Tsangpo remain largely unaffected by human influence due to their extreme isolation.

The Grand Canyon of Tibet serves as a moisture corridor, allowing Indian monsoons to reach the interior of the Tibetan Plateau.

High-altitude glaciers feed into the Yarlung Tsangpo from the surrounding Eastern Himalayan peaks.

The Tsangpo Canyon is home to indigenous Monpa and Lhoba peoples who have lived in the region for centuries.

Geological activity continues to uplift the mountains around the Yarlung Tsangpo Grand Canyon, even as the river carves deeper.

National Geographic sponsored a famous and tragic kayaking expedition to the Tsangpo in 1998 to document its inner depths.

The "Rainbow Falls" of the Yarlung Tsangpo were named by botanist Frank Kingdon-Ward during his 1924 exploration.

The Tsangpo region is often cited as a possible real-world inspiration for the mythical land of Shangri-La.

China established the Yarlung Tsangpo Grand Canyon National Reservation to protect the area's unique biodiversity.

Proposed hydroelectric dams on the Yarlung Tsangpo could potentially produce more energy than any other power plant on Earth.

The Tsangpo Canyon walls are composed of ancient metamorphic rocks that have been exposed by intense river erosion.

Seasonal variations in the Yarlung Tsangpo river flow are extreme, fueled by both snowmelt and heavy summer rains.

Because of its depth, the Yarlung Tsangpo Grand Canyon contains several distinct climate zones stacked vertically.

Thick forests in the lower Tsangpo Gorge transition into alpine shrublands and tundra as the elevation increases.

The Yarlung Tsangpo is considered one of the last great wildernesses remaining on the Asian continent.

Rapid elevation changes in the Tsangpo create some of the most powerful river rapids found anywhere in nature.

Remote sensing and satellite imagery were required to finally confirm the full dimensions of the Yarlung Tsangpo Grand Canyon.

The Tsangpo riverbed sits at an elevation of about 2,900 meters at the start of the gorge and drops to 660 meters near the border.

Scientists study the Yarlung Tsangpo Grand Canyon to understand how tectonic plates and river systems interact over millions of years.

The Tsangpo Canyon receives significantly more rainfall than the arid central Tibetan Plateau to its north.

Large-scale infrastructure projects in the Yarlung Tsangpo area remain controversial due to environmental and geopolitical concerns.

Kayaking the full length of the Tsangpo Gorge is considered one of the most dangerous feats in extreme sports.

The Yarlung Tsangpo Grand Canyon remains a site of ongoing discovery, with new plant and animal species found regularly.

High humidity within the Tsangpo creates frequent fog and mist, adding to its mysterious reputation.
